Breadth-Thrust Signal

Risk-on conviction meter — the bullish mirror of the Equity Defense Dashboard. A grouped four-dimension breadth score with a conditional forward-return study measured against a bootstrap baseline.

How to use this

  • Read the number as conviction, not a trade. A high score means breadth has thrust — many names participating in a turn up — not that today is an entry point. It sizes how much the tape confirms a risk-on turn.
  • Lean on the six-month median, not the headline win rate. On this 2018–2026 sample the durable edge is the lift in six-month median return. The win-rate edge only clears the baseline noise band at the looser ≥1/≥2 thresholds (see the Forward-return study tab); the ≥3 win rate sits inside the band. Do not treat score ≥ 3 as a confirmed standalone trigger.
  • Respect entry discipline. Thrusts are most useful deployed after flat or negative stretches, to avoid buying the peak of an already-strong run — not to chase one.
  • Combine, do not isolate. This is the bullish mirror of the Equity Defense Dashboard. Use it to gauge risk-on conviction alongside your defensive signals, not as a lone timing model.
  • Mind the caveats. Eight years is a thin sample (few fresh events); 111 of 715 former members are unfetchable and drop from history; pre-2018 is excluded. Treat conclusions as suggestive.

How the signal is formed

Methodology

Why four dimensions, not eight signals

The canonical list of "eight breadth-thrust signals" is not eight independent facts. Several are different mathematical views of the same underlying data series, so summing all eight double-counts a single breadth move and overstates conviction. This engine keeps every signal's information but groups them by the data they actually come from — four independent measurement axes — and drops one signal that adds nothing but a fitted threshold.

The double-counting problem

Signals 1, 2, 6 and 7 are all functions of one daily series — the count of advancers versus decliners. The Summation Index (7) is literally the running cumulative sum of the McClellan Oscillator (2); it carries no information the Oscillator does not. So in a naive 0–8 sum, a single strong advance/decline surge can light up four of the eight boxes at once. "Six of eight firing" can be two or three underlying readings wearing six hats — which inflates the apparent corroboration, exactly what a conviction meter must not do.

The grouping rule

OR within a dimension, sum across dimensions. Within a dimension the sub-conditions are OR-ed — they are alternative expressions of the same data, so they count once. Across dimensions the score is summed, because advance/decline, price-vs-MA, new-highs/lows and volume are genuinely independent ways for breadth to confirm a turn. That is what makes the 0–4 score honest: each point is a distinct piece of evidence, not the same evidence re-counted. Nothing is discarded — the engine still records each sub-condition (Zweig, McClellan, Deemer) individually; they simply do not get to vote four times.

Why the Summation Index is dropped entirely

Two reasons. First, redundancy: since it is a transform of the Oscillator, folding it into D1 would change nothing. Second, and decisively, its thrust condition ("cross above +500 from below 0") hinges on a +500 level that is not canonical the way Zweig's 0.40 → 0.615 is. That threshold depends on the breadth universe and regime, and would have to be calibrated from the very data we are testing on — in-sample overfitting. The rule here is explicit: better to exclude it than to fit a threshold to the data (re-introducing it would need an out-of-sample defence). The other seven thresholds are standard, published values, held frozen.

Proxy note: the canonical table refers to NYSE breadth, but this engine computes advance/decline, new highs/lows, % above 50-day MA and up-volume from S&P 500 constituents as a large-cap proxy. The two converge at the breadth extremes the thrust conditions care about and diverge in the middle, which we do not threshold on.
